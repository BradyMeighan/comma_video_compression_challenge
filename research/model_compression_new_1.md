# Extreme compression that preserves gradient landscapes: a technical roadmap

**The most viable path to ~60× compression while preserving ∂loss/∂input gradients is not any single technique but a layered pipeline: tensor decomposition to slash parameter counts without destroying gradient pathways, followed by aggressive quantization of the decomposed factors, finished with entropy coding.** Low-rank and tensor decompositions are fundamentally superior to sparsity for gradient preservation because they perturb all weights slightly rather than zeroing some — and this distinction, confirmed by both theory and the failure modes you've already observed, is the single most important insight for this problem. Several unconventional approaches — hash-grid distillation, SIREN-based implicit representations, and strong lottery tickets — offer alternate routes to the 1.5 MB target with naturally differentiable architectures. The pure memorization constraint (600 frames) is a massive advantage that most compression literature ignores; it makes aggressive overfitting-based approaches viable that would fail in generalization settings.

## Why sparsity kills gradients but low-rank compression doesn't

The failure of unstructured sparsity at 84% (99.3% forward accuracy, seg_dist jumping from 0.007 to 0.263) has a clean theoretical explanation. For a linear layer y = Wx, the input gradient is ∂L/∂x = W^T · (∂L/∂y). Zeroing 84% of weights zeros 84% of entries in the Jacobian W^T, destroying gradient pathways entirely regardless of forward accuracy. Forward accuracy depends on the function value; gradient quality depends on the function's derivative — and these are decoupled properties.

Low-rank approximation W ≈ U_k Σ_k V_k^T is fundamentally different. The **Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem** guarantees that truncated SVD is the optimal rank-k approximation, with gradient error bounded by ‖∂L/∂x_original − ∂L/∂x_compressed‖ ≤ σ_{k+1} · ‖∂L/∂y‖, where σ_{k+1} is the first discarded singular value. Every weight is perturbed by a small, bounded amount. No gradient pathways are destroyed — they're projected onto a k-dimensional subspace, but all input dimensions still receive gradient signal. A 2025 paper ("Lost in Backpropagation") formalized this precisely: backpropagating through a rank-k layer compresses gradients, with **95–99% of gradient norm suppressed** at very low ranks, but the dominant gradient directions are preserved. This graceful degradation contrasts sharply with the cliff-edge behavior of sparsity.

The same logic explains why your student distillation failed. A MobileUNet has completely different weight matrices than an EfficientNet-B2 UNet — different shapes, different singular spectra, different Jacobian structure. Even at 99.75% output agreement, the Jacobians diverge wildly on out-of-distribution trajectory inputs because the architectures compute the same function via different linear algebra. **Architecture mismatch is a gradient-landscape mismatch, not an output mismatch.**

## The compound compression pipeline: SVD + quantization + entropy coding

No single technique reaches 0.5 bits/parameter (required for 23.5M params in 1.5 MB). But compound approaches get there. The math for a representative pipeline on your 9.6M-param SegNet:

**Stage 1 — Tensor decomposition (10–15× parameter reduction).** For convolution kernels, Tucker-2 decomposition replaces each conv layer with three smaller convolutions (1×1 pointwise → small spatial → 1×1 pointwise). For FC layers, truncated SVD replaces m×n matrices with m×k + k×n factors. At 10× compression, the 9.6M-param model becomes ~960K parameters. Fisher-Weighted SVD (FWSVD, ICLR 2022) improves on standard SVD by weighting reconstruction error by parameter importance to the task loss, preserving gradient-critical weights more accurately. Distribution-Aware Tucker decomposition achieves competitive accuracy without any fine-tuning by minimizing error in function space rather than weight space.

**Stage 2 — Quantization of decomposed factors (4–8× additional compression).** The U, Σ, V factors from SVD have cleaner distributions than raw weights, making them more quantization-friendly. SVDQuant (MIT HAN Lab, ICLR 2025) demonstrated this on diffusion models: 4-bit quantization of SVD-decomposed weights achieved **3.6× memory reduction** with preserved visual quality. At INT4 (8× compression from FP32), 960K params become ~480 KB. At INT8 (4×), they become ~960 KB. **Critical: use weight-only quantization (W4A16 or W4A32)** — keep activations in FP16/FP32 during the backward pass. Weight quantization merely uses approximate weight values in the Jacobian computation. Activation quantization creates step functions that block true gradient flow, causing the "gradient masking" effect documented in adversarial robustness literature (Lin et al., "Defensive Quantization," ICLR 2019).

**Stage 3 — Entropy coding (1.3–1.5× additional compression).** Quantized weight indices have non-uniform distributions. Huffman coding provides 20–30% additional compression. Context-adaptive binary arithmetic coding (DeepCABAC, adapted from H.264/HEVC video coding) pushes further, sometimes compressing below the Shannon entropy bound. The combined pipeline: **10× (SVD) × 8× (INT4) × 1.4× (entropy) = 112× total compression**, yielding ~0.85 MB for the SegNet. Even the more conservative 10× × 4× (INT8) × 1.3× = 52× yields ~1.85 MB.

For both models combined, a pipeline of **Tucker/SVD at 10× + INT4 QAT (weight-only) + arithmetic coding** should comfortably land in the 1.2–2.0 MB range. QAT is essential at INT4 — post-training INT4 failed at 63% accuracy, but LSQ (Learned Step Size Quantization, ICLR 2020) demonstrated **3-bit ResNet models matching FP32 accuracy** on ImageNet when trained with learned quantization parameters. For a 600-frame memorization task, INT4 QAT recovery from 63% to 99%+ is nearly certain, and INT3 is highly plausible.

## Tensor Train decomposition: the extreme compression dark horse

Tensor Train (TT) decomposition deserves special attention. Novikov et al. (NeurIPS 2015) compressed fully-connected layers by up to **200,000×** using TT format with only minor error increase. A 1024×1024 weight matrix (1M params) was compressed to just 5 parameters at extreme settings. Tensor Ring (TR) variants achieved >1,000× compression for RNN layers while maintaining classification accuracy. These numbers dwarf what SVD alone can achieve.

TT decomposition reshapes a weight matrix into a higher-order tensor, then decomposes it as a chain of 3D cores with small "bond dimensions." The resulting model is fully differentiable — gradients flow through the chain of small matrix multiplications exactly as through the original layer. ADTN (Adaptive Deep Tensor Networks, 2024) encodes parameter tensors into deep tensor networks achieving extreme compression independent of distillation or quantization, and can be combined with both. QuaDCNN (2025) demonstrated the TT-SVD + quantization compound approach on VGG and ResNet architectures with significant size reduction and minimal accuracy loss.

A realistic pipeline: **TT decomposition at 20× + INT4 quantization at 8× = 160×** total compression, which would put both models combined at ~590 KB — well under the 1.5 MB target with headroom for keeping gradient-sensitive layers at higher precision. The TedNet PyTorch library provides implementations. The risk is that TT decomposition has been demonstrated primarily on FC-heavy architectures, and applying it to the tightly-coupled EfficientNet-B2 UNet with residual/skip/SE connections requires careful layer-by-layer treatment.

## Gradient-preserving training: Sobolev and KDIGA losses

Even with architecture-preserving compression (SVD/TT instead of student distillation), fine-tuning the compressed model to recover gradient quality requires the right loss function. Two techniques from the literature directly address this:

**Sobolev Training** (Czarnecki et al., NeurIPS 2017) trains models to match both function values and derivatives of a target function. Tsay (2021, Computers & Chemical Engineering) applied this specifically to **surrogate models used in gradient-based optimization** — exactly your use case — and demonstrated that Sobolev-trained surrogates produce more accurate derivatives with direct benefits for downstream optimization quality. For high-dimensional pixel inputs, stochastic Sobolev training projects the full Jacobian onto random vectors, making the gradient-matching loss tractable: instead of matching the full 150K×output_dim Jacobian, match J·v for random vectors v. This is the theoretically principled approach.

**KDIGA** (Knowledge Distillation with Input Gradient Alignment, Shao et al., NeurIPS 2022) adds ‖∇_x f_student − ∇_x f_teacher‖₂ to the distillation loss. Critically, it demonstrated **cross-architecture gradient transfer** (ResNet → ViT), proving that input gradient alignment can work even across architectural boundaries. The authors proved that under local linearity assumptions, a KDIGA-trained student achieves at least the teacher's certified robustness. For fine-tuning a compressed same-architecture model on 600 frames with known trajectory data, KDIGA provides a practical loss term.

The recommended fine-tuning loss after compression: **L = L_output + λ₁ · L_Jacobian + λ₂ · L_trajectory**, where L_output matches teacher outputs on the 600 frames, L_Jacobian matches input gradients (via stochastic Sobolev projection), and L_trajectory specifically matches gradients at points along the 4,200 trajectory frames. Since the trajectory is pre-computed, you can weight gradient matching more heavily at trajectory points where optimization sensitivity is highest.

## Unconventional approaches that bypass conventional compression entirely

Three novel architectures merit serious consideration because they naturally fit the 1.5 MB budget while being inherently differentiable:

**Hash-grid + tiny MLP (Instant-NGP style).** This is the top-ranked unconventional approach. The architecture maps (frame_index, pixel_x, pixel_y) → model outputs using multi-resolution hash tables (~1.2 MB) plus a 4-layer, 64-neuron MLP (~12K params). The entire pipeline is differentiable — gradients flow through hash table lookups via trilinear interpolation weights. Hash grids excel at memorization because each entry specializes for a local region. CNC (CVPR 2024) demonstrated **100× compression** of Instant-NGP models using context-based entropy coding. For 600 frames, this architecture can allocate hash table capacity very efficiently. Train with Sobolev loss to match both teacher outputs and gradients. SHACIRA (ICCV 2023) provides compression of hash-grid representations via quantized latents with parameterized decoders.

**SIREN-based implicit neural representation.** SIREN networks (Sitzmann et al., NeurIPS 2020) use periodic (sinusoidal) activations and were explicitly designed to preserve derivatives — they naturally represent signals and their derivatives simultaneously. A SIREN mapping (frame_id, x, y) → (segmentation, pose) could be sized to exactly 1.5 MB. The periodic activations ensure smooth, well-defined gradients at all orders. This is particularly relevant because SIREN was developed in the neural implicit representation community where differentiability through the model is a first-class concern.

**Strong Lottery Tickets (seed + binary mask).** The Strong Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Zhou et al., 2019; Ramanujan et al., 2020) proves that sufficiently overparameterized random networks contain binary subnetworks achieving high accuracy without any weight training. Storage requires only: RNG seed (negligible) + sparse binary mask. At 95% sparsity on 23.5M params, the mask requires ~1.175M bits; with entropy coding, roughly **~150 KB**. The Edge-Popup algorithm finds these subnetworks. The massive advantage: the forward computation uses the same random weights (regenerated from seed), so gradients flow normally. The risk is that extreme sparsity may still damage gradient pathways, though the architecture topology is preserved. This approach is speculative for gradient preservation but extraordinary for raw compression ratio.

## Mixed-precision and codebook quantization fill the precision gap

If the SVD/TT pipeline lands slightly above 1.5 MB, two techniques provide the last mile of compression:

**Mixed-precision quantization** (HAWQ, Dong et al., ICCV 2019) uses Hessian eigenvalue analysis to assign per-layer bit widths. Layers with flat Hessian spectra tolerate INT2–3; sensitive layers (first conv, skip connections, attention) stay at INT8. HAWQ **compressed SqueezeNext to just 1 MB** while maintaining accuracy. For your models, a Hessian sensitivity analysis would identify which EfficientNet-B2 layers are gradient-critical (likely the decoder/skip connections) versus compressible (likely the deep encoder body).

**Codebook (k-means) quantization** outperforms uniform scalar quantization at the same bit budget because centroids adapt to the actual weight distribution. This may explain why INT5 works but INT4 fails — uniform 4-bit quantization wastes levels on sparsely-populated weight ranges, while **k-means with 16 centroids** (same 4-bit index) places levels where weights are densest. Deep Compression (Han et al., ICLR 2016) achieved **35–49× compression** combining pruning + codebook quantization + Huffman coding. Self-Compression (arXiv 2301.13142) learns per-channel bit depths and achieves matching FP32 accuracy with as few as **3% of original bits remaining** (33× from FP32) on CIFAR-10. PV-Tuning (NeurIPS 2024) improves on STE-based codebook learning by alternating continuous codebook optimization with greedy discrete code assignment, achieving the first Pareto-optimal 2-bit quantization for LLMs.

## Actionable implementation roadmap

Based on this research, here are the recommended approaches ordered by expected reward-to-effort ratio:

The **highest-confidence conventional approach** is: (1) Apply Fisher-Weighted SVD or Tucker-2 decomposition layer-by-layer, with rank selection guided by Hessian sensitivity analysis, targeting 10–15× parameter reduction. (2) Fine-tune the decomposed model on 600 frames with Sobolev/KDIGA loss (output matching + stochastic Jacobian matching + trajectory-point gradient matching). (3) Apply QAT with learned step sizes (LSQ) at INT4 weights-only, calibrated on the 600 frames plus trajectory data. (4) Apply arithmetic coding to the quantized factor matrices. Expected result: **1.0–2.0 MB with high gradient fidelity.**

The **highest-potential unconventional approach** is hash-grid + MLP distillation: design an Instant-NGP-style architecture within the 1.5 MB budget, train it with Sobolev loss against both teacher models, and apply CNC-style entropy coding to the hash tables. This sidesteps the compression problem entirely by reframing it as function distillation into a natively compact, differentiable architecture designed for spatial memorization. Expected result: **~1.3–1.5 MB with excellent gradient preservation**, though it requires the most implementation effort.

The **safest incremental improvement** over your current INT5 baseline (13 MB) is: replace uniform scalar quantization with k-means codebook quantization (same 5-bit index budget but non-uniform levels), then add mixed-precision assignment (HAWQ) to push gradient-insensitive layers to 3-bit codebooks while keeping critical layers at 5-bit. With entropy coding, this could reach **4–6 MB** — not 1.5 MB, but a significant step with minimal risk.

## Conclusion

The gradient preservation problem in extreme compression is fundamentally about Jacobian fidelity, not output fidelity — a distinction the mainstream compression literature largely ignores. Three key principles emerge from this research. First, **low-rank decomposition is the natural complement to gradient-sensitive compression** because it provides bounded, smooth weight perturbations with optimal approximation guarantees (Eckart-Young), while sparsity creates discontinuous Jacobian damage. Second, **weight-only quantization preserves input gradients far better than weight+activation quantization** because quantized weights still produce exact linear operations, while quantized activations introduce step-function nonlinearities that block gradient flow. Third, **the 600-frame memorization constraint transforms this from an impossible compression problem into a tractable one** — approaches like hash-grid distillation and task-specific QAT that would fail for general-purpose models become viable when the domain is this narrow. The TT decomposition literature, reporting 200,000× compression on FC layers, suggests that even the aggressive 60× target may have considerable headroom with the right decomposition strategy applied to the right layers.
